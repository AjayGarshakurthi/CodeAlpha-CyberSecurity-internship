🔍 What Is Phishing?

📧

Definition

Phishing is a social engineering attack where criminals impersonate trusted entities to trick victims into revealing sensitive information — passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

📊

By the Numbers

  • 🔴 3.4 billion phishing emails sent daily
  • 🔴 36% of data breaches involve phishing
  • 🔴 $17,700 lost per minute to phishing
  • 🔴 1 in 99 emails is a phishing attempt
🎯

Goal of Attackers

  • Steal login credentials
  • Install malware / ransomware
  • Commit financial fraud
  • Gain corporate network access

🗂️ Types of Phishing Attacks

📧 Email Phishing ▼

The most common form. Attackers send mass emails pretending to be banks, tech companies, or government agencies. They contain malicious links or attachments.

Example: "Your PayPal account has been suspended. Click here."
🎯 Spear Phishing ▼

Highly targeted attacks using personal information gathered from social media or data breaches. The email appears to come from a known contact.

Example: "Hi [Your Name], please review the attached Q3 report — [Your Boss]"
🐋 Whaling ▼

Targets high-profile executives (CEOs, CFOs). Often involves fake legal subpoenas or urgent wire transfer requests.

Example: Fake IRS audit notice sent to a company's CFO.
📱 Smishing (SMS) ▼

Phishing via text messages. Often impersonates delivery companies, banks, or government agencies with urgent calls to action.

Example: "USPS: Your package is held. Confirm address: bit.ly/xxxxx"
📞 Vishing (Voice) ▼

Phone-based phishing. Attackers impersonate tech support, IRS agents, or bank fraud departments to extract information verbally.

Example: "This is Microsoft Support. Your PC has a virus. Give us remote access."
🌐 Pharming ▼

Redirects users from legitimate websites to fake ones by poisoning DNS records or modifying the hosts file — no click required.

Example: Typing "bank.com" takes you to a fake clone site.

🔎 How to Recognize Phishing

📧 Suspicious Email — Hover the red flags
From: security@paypa1.com ⚠️
To: undisclosed-recipients ⚠️
Subject: ⚠️ URGENT: Your account will be SUSPENDED in 24 hours!

Dear Valued Customer ⚠️,

We have detected suspicious activity ⚠️ on your account. You must verify your information immediately or your account will be permanently closed.

Click here to verify your account ⚠️

Regards,
PayPal Security Team ⚠️

👆 Hover over the ⚠️ flags above to learn why each element is suspicious.

Red Flag Checklist

🔴 Mismatched or lookalike sender domain
🔴 Generic greeting ("Dear Customer")
🔴 Urgent / threatening language
🔴 Suspicious or shortened links
🔴 Unexpected attachments
🔴 Requests for passwords or card numbers
🔴 Poor grammar and spelling
🔴 Too-good-to-be-true offers
🔴 Mismatched branding / logos
🔴 No HTTPS on login pages

🧠 Social Engineering Tactics

Attackers exploit human psychology, not just technology. Understanding these tactics is your best defence.

⏰

Urgency & Scarcity

Creating time pressure ("Act in 24 hours!") prevents victims from thinking critically or verifying the request.

🏛️

Authority

Impersonating bosses, IT departments, banks, or government agencies. People are conditioned to comply with authority figures.

😨

Fear & Intimidation

Threatening account closure, legal action, or arrest to override rational decision-making and trigger panic responses.

🤝

Reciprocity

Offering something free (gift card, prize, software) to create a sense of obligation and lower the victim's guard.

👥

Social Proof

"Thousands of users have already updated their accounts." Peer pressure makes the request seem legitimate and normal.

💌

Familiarity & Liking

Using your name, referencing colleagues, or mimicking communication styles from people you trust to build false rapport.

📰 Real-World Phishing Examples

2016

Google & Facebook — $100M BEC Scam

Lithuanian fraudster Evaldas Rimasauskas impersonated a Taiwanese hardware vendor via fake invoices. Both tech giants wired over $100 million before the fraud was discovered.

💡 Lesson: Always verify wire transfer requests via a separate channel.
2020

Twitter Bitcoin Scam

Attackers used spear phishing and vishing to compromise Twitter employee credentials, then hijacked accounts of Obama, Biden, Musk, and Apple to promote a Bitcoin scam — netting $120,000 in hours.

💡 Lesson: Even tech-savvy employees can be socially engineered.
2022

Uber Data Breach

An 18-year-old attacker used MFA fatigue (repeatedly sending push notifications) combined with a WhatsApp message impersonating IT support to gain full network access.

💡 Lesson: MFA alone isn't enough — social engineering can bypass it.
2023

MGM Resorts — $100M Ransomware

ALPHV/BlackCat group used a 10-minute LinkedIn research + vishing call to impersonate an employee to the IT help desk, gaining access that led to a ransomware attack costing MGM over $100 million.

💡 Lesson: Help desk staff need strict identity verification protocols.

✅ Best Practices to Stay Safe

01

Verify Before You Click

Hover over links to preview the real URL. When in doubt, navigate directly to the website by typing the address manually.

02

Use Multi-Factor Authentication

Enable MFA on all accounts. Even if credentials are stolen, attackers can't log in without the second factor. Use hardware keys (FIDO2) when possible.

03

Use a Password Manager

Password managers auto-fill only on the correct domain — they won't fill credentials on a fake lookalike site, acting as a built-in phishing detector.

04

Keep Software Updated

Phishing emails often deliver malware that exploits unpatched vulnerabilities. Enable automatic updates for OS, browsers, and security software.

05

Verify Requests Out-of-Band

If you receive an urgent request from a colleague or executive, call them directly using a known number — never reply to the suspicious email.

06

Report Suspicious Emails

Use your email client's "Report Phishing" button. Forward to reportphishing@apwg.org or your organisation's security team.

07

Check HTTPS — But Don't Trust It Blindly

HTTPS means the connection is encrypted, not that the site is legitimate. Phishing sites can and do use HTTPS. Always verify the full domain name.

08

Limit Your Digital Footprint

Reduce publicly available personal information on social media. Attackers use LinkedIn, Facebook, and Instagram to craft convincing spear phishing emails.
